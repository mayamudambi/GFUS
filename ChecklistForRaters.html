The Criteria for Useful Science

The Criteria for Useful Science Checklist is a checklist developed to rate the value of scientific papers. This checklist was developed with the intention that all stakeholders, and other intelligent adults, can use it. It is not necessary that academics perform the ratings on papers. The below criteria (33 in total) are derived from Armstrong and Green's upcoming paper Guidelines for Science: Evidence and Checklists, which focuses upon the methods by which academics can increase the scientific value of their papers by utilizing the scientific method.

The task: Utilize the below checklist to assess the paper's compliance with each individual criterion of useful science. You, the rater, must be convinvinced of the paper’s usefulness by clear descriptions of the research process, findings, and conclusions. If you are unsure from the researchers’ description whether the research complies, check False / Unclear.

Assess compliance with lettered items under each criterion, below. Do not speculate. Then assess whether criterion 1-7 are true based on compliance with the associated items.

1. Findings are useful (Judged based on title, abstract, result tables, or conclusions)
a) Important problem?
b) Improved understanding of causality, prediction, decision-making, policy, or methods?
c) Findings shown to be surprising to practitioners?
d) Findings shown to be surprising to researchers?
e) Directional findings shown to be surprising?
f) Effect size of findings shown to be surprising?
2. Comprehensive review of prior scientific knowledge?
a) Search procedures shown to be objective with respect to reasonable hypotheses?
b) Search procedures shown to be comprehensive with respect to reasonable hypotheses?
c) Contacted authors who were on cited on substantive matters to ensure that the summary of their findings was correct, the references were correct, and that no key studies were overlooked.
3. Full disclosure
a) Multiple reasonable hypotheses: Prior and revisions (e.g., direction and magnitude of causal effects)?
b) Conditions for hypotheses: Prior and revised?
c) Methods are fully described and easy to understand
d) Data are easily accessible using information provided in the paper
e) Other information needed for understanding (e.g. reviewers if acceptable, acknowledgements, shortcomings, potential biases) provided
4. Data are valid and reliable
a) Data were shown to be relevant to the problem
b) All relevant data (multiple measures) were used to help ensure validity and compensate for biases
c) Reliability of data was assessed
d) Longest available series used when analyzing time series data
5. Methods were valid and simple
a) Methods were shown to be relevant for the problem, unless obvious to all
b) Multiple validated methods were used
c) Methods used cumulative scientfic knowledge explicitly
d) Methods were sufficiently simple for all potential users of the findings to understand
6. Experimental evidence was used to test all reasonable alternative hypotheses
a) All reasonable hypotheses were compared using experimental evidence under explicit conditions
b) Predictive validity of hypotheses were tested using out-of-sample data
7. Conclusions are consistent with the evidence
a) Conclusions are logically consistent with the evidence presented in the paper
b) Conclusions contribute to cumulative scientific knowlege on the problem addessed by the paper
